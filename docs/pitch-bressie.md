# Think First — a conversation for Niall

Hi Niall,

I'm Liam Croash, from Mullingar. My wife Emer Savage was in school with your sister — I mention it only to say I'm not a stranger to your world. We grew up in the same town, with the same shadow of St Loman's in the background.

After last night's show, I suspect you're getting a lot of messages. I'll try to make this one worth reading.

I've spent years working in data and AI. What I'm watching now worries me — not in a doomsday way, but in a quiet, specific way that I think you'd recognise.

Agentic AI is starting to replace the moments at work that were technically inefficient but were actually doing something important. Meetings. The corridor conversation. The ten minutes before a call where you catch up with a colleague as a person. AI is replacing them with focused work, async decisions, data-driven everything. On a spreadsheet, that looks like progress. But those moments were load-bearing. They were where relationships got maintained, where people felt seen, where there was a little breathing room between inputs. Now there isn't. The cognitive capacity that used to have somewhere to rest is fully consumed — and there's nothing left for the people who matter most when work ends.

The people feeling this first and most acutely are in the tech industry. And within that group, there's a significant cohort — disproportionately ADHD, disproportionately autistic — who are already managing a high cognitive load while navigating systems that weren't designed for how they think. They're the ones most directly in the path of this. They're also, not coincidentally, the ones who are best at working with machines when the conditions are right. That's not a contradiction — it's an opportunity, if we approach it correctly.

There's a parallel cohort of kids coming up behind them. Social media and app design spent a decade running an experiment on children's attention without asking permission. We're starting to see the results. Agentic AI is the next chapter of the same story — faster, more pervasive, more personally targeted. Tech is just where it lands first, because nowhere is moving as fast. But it won't stay there.

My fear isn't that AI takes our jobs. It's that it takes our attention, our presence, our ability to actually be with the people we love. It's increasing the speed of living. That's not what it should be for.

I've been building something called Think First. It teaches people to get genuinely fluent with AI — to communicate with it clearly and efficiently, to control what they get back, to leave "machine mode" at work — so the boundary comes back. When working with AI becomes a real skill rather than a constant cognitive tax, people get some of themselves back. It's designed to work for the full range of how people think, including the people who've always had to work harder to be understood by systems that weren't built for them.

I had my own difficult year recently. I know what it feels like when the metrics say everything is fine and something else entirely is happening. That experience is what made this feel urgent rather than just interesting.

My mission is simple: *help people save their mental bandwidth for the people they love.*

I think there's a 3 or 4 part story here — mental health, tech, ADHD and ASD in the workforce, the kids coming up through it — that nobody has told properly yet. I don't know exactly what shape a conversation between us would take, but I'd like to find out. I'd be glad to meet anywhere that suits.

Liam
