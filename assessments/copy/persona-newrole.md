## 3. Module Intro Paragraphs

### B-01: Your First Message

You are about to start a job where people will probably assume you already know how to use this. That assumption is mostly fine — there is not much to know yet. What you need right now is to get one message sent so the tool stops feeling like something you need to figure out before you can use it. This module is a five-minute exercise, not a lesson. You will type something, press send, read what comes back, and that will be the last time you wonder whether you are doing it wrong. Nothing you type here matters — use it as a dry run before you use it for anything real.

### B-02: Copy, Personalise, Use

Once you can send a message, the next thing that matters is writing one that gets you something actually useful. At work, vague questions get vague answers — from people and from AI. This module gives you a template you can fill in for almost any task in your first few weeks: drafting something, summarising something you have been sent, preparing for a meeting. You do not need to write these from scratch. You copy the template, swap in your details, and send it. That is the whole skill, and it works immediately.

### B-04: Your Input Shapes the Output

If you have sent a message to an AI and got back something too long, too shallow, or just not quite right, this module explains why. The AI is not guessing randomly — it is mirroring how much you gave it. A one-line message gets a one-paragraph answer. A message that tells the AI your role, what the output is for, and how long it should be gets something far more useful. In your first month, you will be doing a lot of tasks you have not done before. This module shows you how to get better output faster, without trial and error.

## 4. Exercise Variants

### B-01 Exercise Variant

#### Step 1: Send one message

Copy this message exactly as written:

> I'm starting a new job at a marketing agency next month. What are three things I could do in my first week to make a good impression?

Paste it into the text area on your platform. Press send.

The AI will respond with a list of suggestions. It may be three bullet points. It may be three paragraphs. It may add extra context you did not ask for. Any of those are fine.

That is your first message sent. The AI answered. It did not judge whether the question was good. Nothing broke.

#### Step 2: Now make it yours (optional)

Here is a second message you can try:

> I'm starting a new job at [type of company or team] next month as a [your job title]. What are three things I could do in my first week to make a good impression?

Before you send this one, replace the parts in square brackets with your actual situation. Change "marketing agency" to wherever you are actually going, and "your job title" to what you will actually be doing.

This is the pattern that runs through the rest of the course: start with a message someone else wrote, change the parts that should be yours, send it. B-02 walks through that step in full. For now, just swap those two details and send it.

### B-02 Exercise Variant

#### COPY

Here is a starting message. Copy it exactly as written — brackets and all.

> I'm a [your role] working on [brief description of task]. I need you to [verb: summarise / explain / draft / list] [the specific thing]. Keep it under [length: e.g. 100 words / 5 bullet points]. Don't include [what to leave out].

Do not change anything yet. Just copy it and paste it somewhere you can edit it — a notes app, a text document, or directly into the text area on your AI platform.

#### PERSONALISE

Now go through each bracket, one at a time.

**[your role]**
What you do — your job title, or a short description of it. This gives the AI useful context about your situation.
- Examples: "junior account executive", "marketing assistant", "graduate trainee"

**[brief description of task]**
One sentence about what you are working on right now. Keep it short.
- Examples: "writing a short bio for the company's team page", "preparing for my first client briefing this Thursday"

**[verb: summarise / explain / draft / list]**
Pick the one closest to what you need. Stick to these four for now — they have a clear ceiling and will do only what you asked. You will learn the full set of safe verbs in B-05.
- "summarise" means: make this shorter
- "explain" means: help me understand this
- "draft" means: write a first version of this
- "list" means: give me a set of options

**[the specific thing]**
What do you want that action applied to? Be specific.
- Examples: "a 60-word bio that mentions my degree and that I'm joining the team in May", "the key points I should know before sitting in on a client call"

**[length: e.g. 100 words / 5 bullet points]**
How long should the response be? Pick any size that makes sense for your task.
- Examples: "under 80 words", "5 bullet points", "one short paragraph"

**[what to leave out]**
What do you not want in the response? This is your chance to prevent things that would waste your time.
- Examples: "clichés like 'passionate about' or 'excited to join'", "any questions back to me — just give me a first draft"

#### USE

Here is what it looks like after personalising:

> I'm a marketing assistant working on writing a short bio for my company's team page. I need you to draft a 60-word professional bio in the third person. Keep it under 80 words. Don't include phrases like "passionate about" or "excited to join the team."

Notice: no brackets left. Every placeholder has been replaced with specific, real details. The message sounds like something a person would actually send.

Now do it yourself. Go back to the version you copied, replace each bracket with your own details, and send it.

---

**Three example scenarios:**

**New graduate, account coordinator at an agency:**
> I'm a junior account coordinator working on summarising a client brief I've just been sent. I need you to summarise the brief into 5 bullet points covering the main objectives, target audience, and deliverables. Keep it to 5 bullet points. Don't include the budget details or timelines.

**Graduate trainee, internal comms team:**
> I'm a graduate trainee working on preparing for a team catch-up meeting tomorrow where I need to give a quick update on a project. I need you to draft a two-minute verbal summary of the progress I've made. Keep it under 150 words. Don't include anything that sounds like I'm reading from a script.

**Marketing assistant, e-commerce brand:**
> I'm a marketing assistant working on drafting my first email to a supplier to request their media kit. I need you to draft a short professional email introducing myself and asking for their current rate card and brand assets. Keep it under 100 words. Don't include anything that sounds too formal or stiff — we have a casual brand tone.
