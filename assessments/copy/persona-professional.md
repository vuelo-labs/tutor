## 3. Module intro paragraphs

### B-04: Your Input Shapes the Output

You've probably noticed by now that the same basic request can produce a thorough, usable response one day and something almost uselessly generic the next. That inconsistency usually isn't random. The AI mirrors the level of detail you gave it. A vague request reads as: quick answer expected. A message that explains your role, what the output is for, and what good looks like reads as something different entirely. This module shows you the mechanism, and gives you a template you can use from here on for any document-based task.

### B-05: One Thing at a Time

When you're busy, the natural instinct is to front-load a message with everything you need: summarise the meeting notes, pull out the action items, and draft the follow-up email. It feels efficient. In practice, what you get back is a rough attempt at all three — none of them quite right, and all of them still needing work. Sending tasks separately takes more clicks. It saves time overall, because each response comes back clean enough to actually use. This module shows the difference, using the kind of multi-part requests that come up constantly in client work.

### B-06: Tell It When to Stop

Left to its own judgment, the AI will decide how long your answer should be. That judgment is often wrong for professional use: too long for a client-facing summary, too short for a detailed briefing note, structured in a way that doesn't match how your firm writes. A stopping condition — a simple instruction about length, format, or what not to include — changes that. This module teaches you to add one to every message, which is the difference between a response you can paste and a response you have to reformat.

## 4. Exercise variants

### B-04 exercise variant

**The scenario**
You've just come out of a client steering committee. There are meeting notes in your inbox. You need a summary for a status report that goes to a senior stakeholder who wasn't in the room.

---

**Step 1: Send Version A (the message most people actually send)**

Copy this and send it with your meeting notes pasted at the end:

> Summarise these meeting notes for me: [paste your meeting notes here]

Send it. Read what comes back.

---

**Step 2: Start a new conversation and send Version B**

Open a new conversation. Copy this message, fill in the brackets, and paste the same notes at the end:

> I'm a senior account manager preparing the monthly status report for [client name]. Summarise these meeting notes in 4 bullet points covering decisions made, open issues, and agreed next steps. Write for a [Partner / Director / CFO — pick the one that fits] who wasn't in the meeting. Keep each bullet under 25 words. Don't include background context we've already covered in previous reports.
>
> [paste the same meeting notes here]

Send it. Read what comes back.

---

**Step 3: Compare**

Look at both responses. Which one could go directly into your report? Which one would you spend ten minutes editing? The difference is not that one used better technology. It's that one gave the AI a clear picture of what the output was for.

---

**COPY — PERSONALISE — USE**

Here is the Version B template, clean:

> I'm a [your role] preparing [what this output is for]. Summarise these [meeting notes / document / report] in [format — e.g. 4 bullet points / a short paragraph] covering [the specific angle]. Write for [intended reader]. Keep it under [length]. Don't include [what to leave out].
>
> [paste your document here]

**How to personalise it:**

**[your role]** — your actual title or function. "Senior account manager," "project lead," "engagement director." This sets the context for the whole response.

**[what this output is for]** — one sentence. "The monthly status report," "a client briefing ahead of next week's review," "the internal handover note."

**[format]** — how you want it shaped. "4 bullet points," "a short paragraph," "an executive summary under 150 words."

**[the specific angle]** — what matters for this purpose. "Decisions made and next steps," "risks and open issues," "commercial implications."

**[intended reader]** — who will read this. The AI will adjust the level of assumed knowledge accordingly.

**[length]** — a hard limit. "Under 150 words," "no more than 5 bullets," "one sentence per point."

**[what to leave out]** — anything that would waste your reader's time. "Background context already covered," "actions not yet confirmed," "any caveats."

### B-05 exercise variant

**The scenario**
You've just finished a client engagement review. You have a briefing document to work from. You need three things: a summary for the client file, a draft email to the client sharing the outcome, and a list of internal follow-up actions for your team.

---

**Step 1: Send everything at once**

Copy this message, fill in the brackets, and send it:

> Can you summarise this engagement review, then draft a short client email sharing the key outcomes, and also pull out the internal follow-up actions for my team? The document is: [paste your briefing document here]

Send it. Read through the whole response. Notice which of the three pieces actually works, which is generic, and which has missed the point.

---

**Step 2: Start a new conversation and send them one at a time**

Open a new conversation. Send these three messages in sequence, reading each response before moving to the next.

**Message 1:**

> Summarise this engagement review in 5 bullet points. Cover: the original scope, what was delivered, any significant issues, and the client's stated satisfaction. Keep each bullet under 20 words.
>
> [paste your briefing document here]

Read the summary. Does it reflect what actually happened? If a bullet is wrong or missing something, ask for a correction now — before you move on.

**Message 2 (once you're satisfied with the summary):**

> Using that summary, draft a short email to the client confirming the engagement outcome. Professional tone. Under 120 words. No next steps yet — this is acknowledgement only. Start with the result, not the process.

Read it. Check the tone is right for your client relationship. Adjust if needed.

**Message 3 (once you're happy with the email):**

> Now list the internal follow-up actions from that engagement review. Bullet points, owner field left blank. Flag anything with a deadline. Keep it to confirmed actions only — no suggestions.

Read it.

---

**Step 3: Compare**

Put the three outputs from Step 2 next to what Step 1 produced. Which version of the client email is actually sendable? Which action list would you trust to brief your team from? The extra two messages cost you under a minute. What they bought you is outputs that don't need rebuilding.
