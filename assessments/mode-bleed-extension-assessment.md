# Assessment: Extending the mode-bleed concept

**Context:** The mode-bleed paragraph was added to the manifesto following the previous assessment (mode-bleed-assessment.md). Draft A was implemented verbatim in both the browsable manifesto and the downloadable PDF version. A follow-up sentence was also added: "Be mindful of what mode you are in after a session with machines. And before you walk back into a human one." Video Brief 3 already picks up the concept, including the note that mode bleed works "in both directions" at work too.

Three connected ideas are now proposed for further extension. This assessment evaluates each one against the site's existing content, voice, audience, and course integrity.

---

## Idea A: Broaden mode-bleed to work relationships

**The proposal:** Make it explicit that mode bleed affects colleagues, not just partners and kids. A colleague asking for help deserves human mode. The "be the change" angle: how you show up models behaviour for others.

### Does it dilute or add?

It dilutes. Here is why.

The current mode-bleed paragraph works because it is precise and domestic. The examples are specific: "Someone asks how you're doing and you give them a status update. Your kid tells you a long, winding story and you're waiting for the point. Your partner needs the slow, circular, going-nowhere conversation — the one that IS the relationship — and you're trying to get to the action item." Every image is intimate. The reader sees their own kitchen, their own sofa, their own child. That specificity is why it lands.

Broadening to "all relationships including colleagues" turns a sharp observation into a general principle. General principles are easier to agree with and harder to feel. "Be human with your colleagues too" is sensible advice. But it does not cut the way "your partner needs the slow, circular, going-nowhere conversation" cuts. The manifesto's power comes from saying one precise thing, not from covering all the contexts where that thing might apply.

The "be the change" framing is a separate problem. The manifesto's voice is confessional and observational. It describes what is happening and trusts you to draw your own conclusions. "If you treat people humanly, you give them permission to do the same" crosses into aspirational territory — it tells you what to become, not what you might be failing to notice. That is a different register. The manifesto has so far avoided it. The closest it gets is "Be mindful of what mode you are in after a session with machines," which is still observational — it says "notice," not "inspire."

Video Brief 3 already handles the work angle, noting that mode bleed goes "in both directions: being too machine with humans at work too. Bullet-pointing conversations that need warmth. Putting time limits on disagreements. Treating emotional requests like structured tasks." That is the right place for it. The video can afford to expand and illustrate in ways the manifesto cannot, because the video is a conversation with a presenter, not a written text that gains power from compression.

### Verdict: Do not implement in the manifesto.

The work-relationships angle is already present in Video 3's brief. The manifesto does not need it, and adding it would weaken what is currently a paragraph that works precisely because it is narrow. If the work angle needs to be more prominent, Video 3 is where it lives. The "be the change" framing should not appear anywhere — it introduces a prescriptive, aspirational register that conflicts with the site's observational voice.

---

## Idea B: Keep the message simple, let implications breathe

**The proposal:** Rather than extending mode-bleed to work, culture-setting, and every other context, trust the reader to draw their own implications. The core message (two modes, the bleed costs you) is already powerful. Restraint may serve it better than enumeration.

### What the existing content suggests

This is not just a reasonable instinct — it is what the site already does, and does well.

The manifesto's structure is instructive. It never lists every application of its ideas. It says "Two modes. Completely different. Know which one you're in. That's the whole skill," and then it stops. It does not enumerate: at home, at work, with friends, with your manager, in meetings, in emails. The reader fills in their own context. That is why it feels personal rather than prescriptive.

The mode-bleed paragraph follows the same pattern. It gives three specific domestic images and names the phenomenon. It does not then say "and this also happens at work, and in your friendships, and in how you lead your team." It trusts the reader. The reader, having recognised their own kitchen, will recognise their own office too — without being told to.

The site's voice is built on this restraint. Consider the manifesto's ending: "The machine is fine. Go be human." That is two sentences doing the work of a paragraph. The site has consistently chosen to say less and let it resonate. Every time it has been tempted to add more context, more caveats, more applications, the right answer has been to resist.

The question is not whether the implications exist. They obviously do. The question is whether the site's job is to name them all or to hand the reader a lens and trust them to look through it. The site has chosen the latter, and that choice is a large part of why it works.

### Verdict: This is the correct instinct. Follow it.

The manifesto is already doing the right thing. The mode-bleed paragraph is complete as written. The implications — work, culture, leadership, friendship — are visible to anyone who has felt the domestic version. Enumerating them would reduce the reader's sense of discovery and replace it with a checklist. The site's voice is at its most powerful when it names one precise thing and leaves space.

## Idea C: A practical 1-pager — coming up / coming down

**The proposal:** A separate page (not course content) with reflection questions for before and after an AI session. Before: "What am I about to work on? Am I clear on what I want? Have I done the human thinking first?" After: "What mode am I in right now? Who am I about to talk to? Do I need to come down before I do?"

### Genuine value or feature creep?

This is the hardest of the three ideas to assess, because it has genuine merit and genuine risk in roughly equal measure.

**The case for it:**

The site's practical guide has a framework (Think, Collab, Dispatch, Iterate) and a set of principles (eight things worth knowing). Both are about how you interact with the machine. Neither addresses the transition moments — the coming up into machine mode and the coming down out of it. The mode-bleed paragraph in the manifesto names the problem ("you forgot to switch back") but offers no mechanism for the switch. The guide's "Pause" callout says "Walk, eat, talk to people" but frames rest as a cognitive strategy, not as a deliberate mode transition.

A short set of questions — genuinely short, five or six lines — that helps someone notice their mode before they sit down and after they stand up would be the practical expression of the manifesto's most personal idea. It would not teach a new skill. It would operationalise the skill the site already teaches: noticing which mode you are in.

The "before" questions are essentially a compressed version of the Think step: have I done the human thinking? Am I clear? That is redundant with the guide. But the "after" questions are genuinely absent from the site. Nothing on the site currently helps the reader make the transition back to human mode. The manifesto says "be mindful." The guide says "pause." Neither gives you a concrete thing to do.

**The case against it:**

The site has a clean information architecture: Manifesto (why), Booklet (what), Handbook (how), Course (practice). A 1-pager about mode transitions does not fit neatly into any of these. It is not philosophy (manifesto), not principles (booklet), not technique (handbook), and not a module (course). It is a self-reflection tool. That is a new category.

Adding a new category to a site with a clear layered structure is a real cost. The homepage currently says "Four layers. Each one builds on the last. Stop when you have what you need." A fifth thing that is not a layer but a tool breaks that clean shape. Where does it go in the navigation? How does a reader find it? What is it called?

There is also a voice problem. The "after" questions — "What mode am I in right now? Who am I about to talk to? Do I need to come down before I do?" — sound like a mindfulness exercise. The previous assessment of this territory (mindfulness-fit-assessment.md) likely flagged similar concerns. The site's voice is conversational, confessional, and observational. A set of self-reflection prompts is a different register — it is instructional and introspective in a way the site has not been.

**Who would use it?**

Someone who has read the manifesto, recognised mode bleed in their own life, and wants a practical anchor. That is a real person. But it is a narrow one — someone who has already done the philosophical work and is looking for a habit tool. The site's primary audience (low-tech-confidence learners being asked to use AI at work for the first time) is not yet at this point. They are still learning to write their first prompt. Mode transitions are a concern for the person six months into regular AI use, not the person in week one.

**Format considerations:**

If it were to exist, the strongest format would be a downloadable card or a small addition to the booklet (which is already designed to be printed and pinned up). A separate page on the site introduces navigational complexity for a very small piece of content. A card that lives inside the booklet preserves the existing architecture while putting the questions where they would actually be used — on a desk, next to a screen.

It should not be course content. The course is 26 modules of practical AI communication skills. A module on "how to come down from machine mode" would be a jarring tonal shift from modules that teach you how to write a role instruction or define done. It belongs in the philosophy layer, not the practice layer.

### Verdict: Conditional — but not as a page.

The "after" questions have genuine value. The "before" questions are redundant with Think. If this idea moves forward, the strongest execution would be:

1. Add two or three lines to the existing "Pause" callout in the guide, turning it from a rest reminder into a mode-transition reminder. Something observational, not instructional: "Before you stand up: which mode are you in? Who are you about to talk to?" This keeps it inside existing content rather than creating a new page.

2. Alternatively, add a small "mode switch" panel to the booklet (the printable PDF), which is already designed as a desk reference. A few questions on the back page. No new URL, no new navigation item, no new content category.

Do not create a standalone page. The site's architecture does not need a fifth layer, and the content is too small to justify one.

## Course integrity

None of these three ideas belongs in the course.

The course is 26 modules of practical AI communication skills for an audience with low general tech confidence. The audience profile is explicit: "Write for someone using AI because they have to, not because they love it. Goal is competence and confidence, not enthusiasm." These are people learning to write their first message, not people reflecting on how AI has changed their relationships.

Mode bleed is a philosophical observation about the cost of machine-mode thinking. It is important. It is true. But it is not a skill you can teach in a module with a Copy-Personalise-Use exercise. There is no prompt to edit. There is no output to evaluate. It is a noticing, not a doing.

The course's job is to make someone competent and confident with AI communication. The manifesto's job is to make them thoughtful about what that competence costs. Those are different jobs, and the site is right to keep them separate.

Video 3 is the one place where mode bleed belongs in taught content, and the brief already handles it well — including the work-relationships angle. That is enough.

---

## Right scope for philosophical depth

The manifesto is currently doing its job well. It makes one clear argument (two modes, know which one you are in), illustrates the cost (depletion and mode bleed), grounds it in personal experience (the wobble, the nine months), and ends with a simple invitation (go be human). It does not over-explain. It does not moralise. It does not tell you how to fix your relationships.

The mode-bleed paragraph was the right addition. It completed the argument by naming the second failure mode — contamination, not just depletion. The manifesto now covers both directions: wasting human energy on machines, and carrying machine energy to humans.

There is no gap remaining that these extensions would fill. The work-relationships angle is covered in the video briefs. The restraint instinct (Idea B) is already the site's practice. The mode-transition questions (Idea C) have a narrow practical value that could be addressed with a few lines added to existing content rather than a new page.

The site's philosophical depth is proportionate to its purpose. It says enough to change how you think about your relationship with AI tools. It does not say so much that it becomes a philosophy project. That proportion is correct.

## Overall recommendation

The manifesto's mode-bleed paragraph is complete as written. It does not need to grow. The right move is to leave the manifesto alone, let Video 3 carry the work-relationships angle as planned, and — if the mode-transition idea still feels important — find a home for it inside existing content (the guide's Pause callout or the booklet) rather than creating anything new.

The strongest version of the site is the one that says the precise thing and stops. That is what it already does.
