# Assessment: Adding "AI can be compulsive/addictive" content

**Proposed addition:** Narrative content addressing the idea that AI use can become compulsive or time-consuming, that people should be mindful of how much time they spend with AI tools, and that awareness of this is part of using AI well.

**Assessed by:** Editorial review against existing site content, voice, audience, and argument structure.

---

## 1. Thematic fit

**The site already says this. Explicitly. It is one of its central arguments.**

The manifesto is not subtle about it. The author describes his own experience in exactly these terms:

> "Then I started working with AI. And it brought back a lot of familiar feelings. Same pattern. Different costume. The machine was always ready for more. Always had energy. Always said 'what's next?' So I kept going. And the whole time it felt like progress. That's the trap. The machine makes burning out feel productive."

That is a first-person account of compulsive AI use. The word "addictive" is not used, but the mechanism is described precisely: the machine's infinite availability creates a pull that feels like productivity but is actually depletion.

The manifesto's entire second half is about stopping. "Rest is your actual job." "The machine doesn't mind waiting." "Go be human." The practical guide includes a standalone pause callout: "The machine doesn't need breaks. You do." Both the E-07 closing and the A-08 closing use the same refrain: "The machine will wait. It does not mind. Go do the work."

The concern is not absent. It is structural. It runs through every layer of the site.

## 2. Voice fit

**An "AI is addictive, be careful" framing would clash badly with the site's voice.**

The site's voice works because it never moralises. It observes. It describes what is happening to you and lets you recognise yourself in it. The manifesto does not say "you should stop overusing AI" — it says "you spent your energy on a machine that didn't need it." The difference is enormous. One is a warning label. The other is a mirror.

The word "addictive" carries clinical and cautionary weight. It positions the speaker as someone who knows better, warning the reader about a danger. That is the opposite of how this site talks. The site says: here is what is happening, here is why it costs you, here is what you can do instead. It treats the reader as someone who will make good decisions once they can see clearly — not someone who needs to be warned.

Adding explicit "be mindful of your screen time" language would sound like a wellness blog grafted onto a site that has carefully avoided being one.

## 3. Audience fit

**The audience is people trying to get started with AI, not people who are overusing it.**

The course begins with "Your First Message" — a module for people who have never sent a message to a chatbot. The audience profile assumes low general tech confidence. These are people who need to be told that nothing will break when they press send.

A "don't overuse AI" message presupposes that the reader is already deep enough in AI use to have developed a compulsive relationship with it. The beginner and enabled-user audience is not there yet. For them, the risk is not overuse — it is not using the tool at all, or using it badly and giving up.

Telling someone who just learned to send their first message that AI can be addictive is like putting a "drink responsibly" label on someone's first glass of water. It addresses a problem they do not have, and it risks making the tool feel more dangerous and fraught than the site wants it to feel.

The advanced audience (Claude Code users) is closer to the right audience for this message, but even there, the site's approach is already calibrated: "set a budget before delegating," "do not re-delegate immediately," "the correction happens in the brief, not in the conversation." These are practical guardrails against overuse — without ever framing the problem as addiction.

## 4. Placement fit

**There is no natural home for a standalone "AI is addictive" section.**

- **Homepage:** The homepage is a hook — it needs to pull people in, not warn them off. Wrong place.
- **Manifesto:** The manifesto already contains the personal burnout narrative. Adding a separate "addiction" section would be redundant and would dilute the manifesto's precise argument (the cost is cognitive and relational, not medical).
- **Guide:** The guide is practical — how to prompt, how to structure, when to stop. A mindfulness message would break its rhythm. The "Pause" callout already exists and does the job without moralising.
- **A course module:** A standalone module on AI addiction would be wildly out of proportion with the course's scope and would shift the entire tone from practical to therapeutic.

The only placement that could work is the one the site already uses: woven into closing moments. "The machine will wait. It does not mind." That line appears at the end of E-07 and A-08. It is the site's version of this message, and it works precisely because it is not labelled as a warning.

## 5. Risk of dilution

**High. This addition would blur the site's most distinctive argument.**

The site's core thesis is unusually specific: you are spending human energy on a machine that does not need it. That is not an argument about addiction or screen time or digital wellness. It is an argument about misallocated effort. The distinction matters.

"Don't overuse AI" is a different claim from "stop performing human communication rituals at a machine that cannot receive them." The first is about quantity. The second is about quality. The site is entirely about the second.

Adding an "AI can be addictive" narrative introduces a quantity argument into a site built on a quality argument. It would make the site's position less distinctive — more like the dozens of "digital wellness" takes already out there — and would undermine the precision that makes the manifesto land.

## 6. Is there a version that works?

**Not as new content. The site already has the best version of this idea.**

The manifesto's burnout narrative, the "Pause" callout in the guide, and the recurring "the machine will wait" refrain are the right version of this idea for this site. They address the same concern — that people can lose themselves in AI interaction — but through the site's own lens: energy, not addiction; efficiency, not abstinence; awareness, not caution.

If there is any gap, it is minor: the manifesto's burnout narrative is personal (the author's story) and the guide's pause callout is brief. There is no moment in the course itself where a learner is invited to reflect on their own relationship with the tool's availability. But even this is a stretch — the course is practical and task-focused, and a reflective prompt about compulsive use would feel foreign in that context.

If you forced me to write one sentence that fits, it would go in the guide's pause callout, extending what is already there:

> "The machine will always be ready for more. That is not an invitation. That is just how machines work."

But honestly, the existing callout already communicates this. The addition is marginal at best.

---

## Summary

The proposed concern is valid and real. But the site already addresses it — thoroughly, in its own voice, through its own lens. The manifesto is a first-person account of exactly this problem. The guide has a dedicated pause callout. The course closes every major stage with "the machine will wait." The idea is not missing. It is load-bearing.

Adding an explicit "AI is addictive" narrative would:
- Introduce clinical/cautionary language into a site that carefully avoids it
- Address a problem the primary audience does not yet have
- Dilute the site's distinctive quality-of-interaction argument with a generic quantity-of-use argument
- Duplicate content the manifesto already handles better

---

**VERDICT: DO NOT IMPLEMENT**

The site already contains the best version of this idea, expressed in its own voice, distributed across the manifesto, the guide, and the course closings. Adding a standalone "AI is addictive" narrative would be redundant at best and tonally damaging at worst. The existing content should be left as it is.
