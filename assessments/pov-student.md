# First browse — honest notes from someone who really needed this

## 1. First impressions

Okay, I'll be honest — the headline stopped me. "You apologised to the chatbot, didn't you." It's not a question. It's a statement. And my immediate reaction was a kind of hot embarrassment because yes, I absolutely have done that. I've typed "sorry if this is a dumb question" into ChatGPT multiple times. I didn't think anyone else noticed or cared. Seeing it named like that, on a website, in the title tag — it felt like being caught out, but in a way that wasn't unkind. More like someone winking at you.

The page itself felt calm and a bit serious, not flashy. The tone in the hero quote — "The skill isn't learning to prompt better. It's just noticing which mode you're in. Once you see it, you can't unsee it." — felt like something a thoughtful person would say, not a marketing line. It didn't feel like it was talking down to me, which a lot of tech content does.

The two mode cards side by side were genuinely useful even on the homepage. I immediately recognised both. The warm card — "Hey! Hope you're having a good day ☺ I was just wondering..." — I've sent that exact message to ChatGPT. And the cool card with "task: defined / context: relevant only / output: specified / emotion: not required" made me laugh, because I have a friend who texts like that and I always find it weirdly cold.

Did it feel like it was for me? Mostly yes. The language didn't assume I knew things. The "2–3 min" note next to the "Give it a go" button was a small but meaningful detail — it told me this wouldn't swallow my evening. I felt like it was designed for someone in a hurry who's slightly anxious. That's me right now.

The one thing that gave me a brief pause: I saw links to "The Manifesto" and "The Booklet" and "The Handbook" in the footer sections. That's a lot of documents. I briefly worried this was one of those sites where the real content is hidden behind multiple layers and you have to read three PDFs before you can do anything useful. I clicked "Give it a go" instead of reading any of those, which is probably what I was supposed to do.

---

## 2. The /see experience

This was the best bit. "Two modes. One skill." and then "You've been talking to machines like they're people. Here's what that's costing you — and the only thing you need to do differently." That sentence — "here's what that's costing you" — landed. Because I've been getting back these long, waffy AI responses and not really understanding why, and I've been half-assuming it's because I'm doing something fundamentally wrong that I'd need to go on a course to fix. Apparently it's just one thing.

The side-by-side panels on the See screen were clear. I didn't need to think hard about either example. The warm panel — the message asking for help with data, with the apology and the "only if you have time" and the emojis — I've sent something that looks almost exactly like that, word for word. The caption underneath it — "A complete social performance — for something that was just waiting for the question" — was the right note to put there. Not mean, just accurate.

The recognition quiz made me feel more confident, not less. I got the first three right without hesitating. The fourth one — the structured one with "context: billing error" — was obvious. The fifth one I actually paused on: "Can you just write me something quick — like a short paragraph — that explains why the project is behind? Keep it simple, nothing fancy." I read it and thought: that sounds casual, so maybe it's human mode? But I clicked machine mode because it has a task and a format and a length, and I was right. The feedback confirmed it: "The conversational wrapper doesn't change what it is." That's the kind of line that makes something click. I would probably use those exact words if someone asked me to explain it.

The final screen — "That's it." followed by "Go walk. Go eat. Go talk to someone. Go be done for the day." — was unexpected and I liked it. Everything online is trying to keep you engaged. This was literally telling me to go away. It felt like the site trusted me, which is not a feeling I usually get from onboarding flows.

One small thing: the transition from "Two modes. One skill." on the intro screen to the actual See step was slightly abrupt. There's a "Begin" button, you click it, and you're suddenly looking at two message examples. I didn't know I was about to see examples — I half-expected a video or a longer explanation first. It was fine once I was in it, but for one second I wasn't sure what I was looking at.

## 3. The course index

When I saw the three stages — Beginner, Enabled User, Advanced Practitioner — my first instinct was to scan to the bottom to see how bad it gets. Advanced Practitioner says "Nine modules for working with Claude Code: cost awareness, session hygiene, context management, tool selection, and agent delegation." I did not know what most of those words meant in this context, and I felt a brief spike of that specific anxiety where you think everyone else probably does know what those mean and you're behind.

But then I looked at the callout note under Advanced Practitioner: "This track requires Claude Code, Anthropic's command-line tool." I don't know what a command-line tool is. That's when I realised this section just isn't for me yet, and that's actually fine — it said so. It wasn't gate-keeping in a mean way, it was labelling, like a sign on a door that says "staff only." I wasn't being judged for not being staff.

The Beginner section was immediately reassuring. Ten modules. The first one is literally called "Your First Message" and the objective is: "Send a message to an AI, read the response, and know that nothing broke." I actually found that funny — not in a condescending way, but in a "they know exactly who's reading this" way. That's genuinely what I was worried about. That something would break. That I'd press send and something bad would happen. Seeing it named so bluntly was calming.

The "Start here" button pointing to B-01 meant I didn't have to make a decision about where to begin. That was good. I don't want decisions at this stage. I just want to be told what to do.

Enabled User also looked like something I'd want to reach. "The First Word Matters" and "Tell It What to Leave Alone" — those titles are specific and tell me exactly what I'll get. They don't sound like corporate training modules. They sound like someone who's been annoyed by this figured out what the fix is.

## 4. B-01 and B-02

B-01 is the most confident piece of writing I've read about AI for beginners, and I mean that as a compliment. "You do not need to know anything about AI to do this. You do not need to be good at technology." Nobody ever says that. Every other resource I've looked at assumes you're already slightly comfortable, and then adds a section at the top that says "no experience required" as a formality. This one actually meant it.

The exercise is asking me to send "What is the capital of France?" That is almost insultingly simple, and it is exactly right. The point is not the question. It says that: "The AI will respond with something about Paris. It may include extra information. It may be a single sentence. It may be a paragraph. Any of those are fine." That section specifically — anticipating the anxiety that comes from a response not looking like what you expected — is genuinely thoughtful. I've had that experience. I sent something and got back an essay when I expected a sentence, and I wasn't sure if I'd done something wrong.

The bit about message limits — the "If it asks you to upgrade" section — would have been so useful to me four months ago. I thought I'd broken ChatGPT the first time I hit a limit. Having the specific platforms listed, with plain explanations of what the limit is and when it resets, is exactly the kind of detail that a person without tech confidence needs and usually has to google for 20 minutes.

B-02 built on B-01 in the right order. The COPY / PERSONALISE / USE structure is clear and memorable. I liked that the template message already looked like something I might actually send at work — not a toy example, but "I'm a small business owner working on replying to a negative customer review." That feels adjacent to my world. (Marketing agency. Clients. Replies to things. Yes.)

The verb breakdown — "summarise means: make this shorter / explain means: help me understand this / draft means: write a first version of this / list means: give me a set of options" — is so useful. I never thought about what verb I was using. I've been typing "can you help me write..." which now feels vague. I understand why having four specific options with clear ceilings is better than having infinite vague ones.

Would I come back tomorrow for B-03? I think so, but only because the "Next" section at the bottom of B-02 told me exactly what B-03 is: "B-03 walks you through the main features of your AI platform so you know where everything is." That's a concrete promise. I know I need that. I don't always find the New Chat button on the first try. The promise of knowing where everything is would get me back.

What might stop me: if I had a busy week and didn't think the next one was urgent enough. There's no nudge beyond the written "Next" link. I'd have to remember to come back on my own.

## 5. What I almost missed

The data note at the bottom of B-01. "Before you paste work documents or other sensitive content into any AI tool, check the platform's data policy." I nearly scrolled past it because the module felt over — the exercise was done, the "Next" link was visible. But I actually stopped and read it, and it's important. My new job is at a marketing agency. There will be client briefs. There will be sensitive stuff. Knowing that free plans might use your conversations for training, and knowing to ask IT before pasting anything sensitive — that's the kind of thing that could actually protect me in a professional context, and it was positioned right where it needed to be, after I'd already done the exercise and felt confident.

Also: the caption "// your friend is not a REST API" on the cool panel in /see. I don't know what a REST API is but I understood what it meant. That was a joke aimed at people who do know what it means, and it still landed for me. That's good writing.

---

## 6. What's missing

One thing that would make this obviously for someone like me: a single sentence somewhere near the top of the course index — or even on the homepage — that acknowledges the work context specifically. Something like "Starting a new job where everyone uses AI? You're in the right place." Right now the framing is very "humans using AI in general" — the apologising to chatbots, the two modes framework — and that framing works, but it doesn't tell me that this course is relevant to my actual Monday morning. The B-02 example about the small business owner replying to a review is the closest thing to a professional context, and it was good, but it arrived at module two. I spent the homepage and the /see experience not being completely sure if this was for hobbyist curiosity or for actual work.

I'd have stayed more certainly from the start if there had been one grounding line that said: this is for people who are about to use AI at work and don't feel ready. That's me, and I found my way here eventually, but I nearly went and looked for something more explicitly "professional" first.
