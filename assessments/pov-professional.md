## First-person assessment — 41-year-old senior account manager, one month into mandatory AI use

**1. The headline test**

"You apologised to the chatbot, didn't you."

Yes I did. Last Tuesday, actually. I wrote "Sorry, I know this is a lot to ask" to the firm's AI tool before pasting in a client brief. I know that's ridiculous. I knew it was ridiculous when I did it.

So the headline landed. It landed hard enough that I felt slightly called out, but it also told me in about four seconds that whoever made this has actually watched normal people use these things. Not annoyed. Slightly wary — I have been burned by things that start well and then turn into a sales pitch or a tech lecture.

**2. Does it feel like it's for me**

More than anything else I have seen on this subject, yes. But it is close to the line in places.

What included me: The "apology reflex" example — "Hi! Hope you're having a good one. So sorry to bother you — I know this is probably a silly question..." That is literally how I talk to ChatGPT. That is mortifying and also exactly why I kept reading.

The line "Nobody has ever hurt a chatbot's feelings" — that is the kind of thing I needed someone to just say out loud a month ago.

The framework — Think, Collab, Dispatch, Iterate — does not assume I know anything about prompting or tokens or models. It assumes I know how to think about a task, which I do. I have been doing that for fifteen years. That felt respectful.

What made me hesitate: The course index has a Stage 3 called "Advanced Practitioner" that mentions "Claude Code," "subagent delegation," and "context compaction." I do not know what any of those words mean. For a moment I wondered if the whole thing was secretly for developers and I was just being eased in gently before hitting a wall. The fact that Stages 1 and 2 exist separately helped, but the Advanced section sitting right there on the same page gave me a flash of "this is going to get technical and leave me behind."

**3. The experience (/see)**

I did it. Took maybe three minutes.

It was not condescending. The two-panel thing — human mode versus machine mode — is genuinely clarifying. The left panel shows someone writing to a chatbot the way I write to a chatbot. The right panel shows someone talking to their friend like a database query. The caption says "your friend is not a REST API." I do not know what a REST API is but I understood the point perfectly.

The quiz was simple enough that I got them all right, which felt good rather than patronising. One was deliberately tricky — something that sounded casual but was actually a machine-mode instruction. That caught me for a second and made me think. That is the right kind of difficulty.

The ending: "The machine will wait. Go be somewhere else. Go walk. Go eat. Go talk to someone." That is a strange thing for a tech product to say. I appreciated it. It felt written by someone who has noticed that people like me feel pressured and anxious about all of this, and was deliberately giving permission to slow down.

**4. B-01 — "What is the capital of France?"**

I have been using this thing at work for a month. So yes, "What is the capital of France?" is too basic for me as an exercise.

But I understand why it starts there. "You do not need to know anything about AI to do this. You do not need to be good at technology." That is written for my colleague Margaret who is 57 and has not even opened the tool yet. If she asked me what to do, I would send her this page. It is the gentlest possible on-ramp and it does not talk down.

My problem is not B-01. My problem is that I need what is probably in B-04 through B-10 and the Enabled User section, but I have to either skim through modules I do not need or guess which one to skip to. There is no "I have already used AI, start here" path.

**5. The moment I decided to stay**

I decided to stay on the homepage, about fifteen seconds in, when I read: "The skill isn't learning to prompt better. It's just noticing which mode you're in. Once you see it, you can't unsee it."

Every other resource I have looked at frames it as: you need to learn a new skill called prompting. This framed it as: you already know how to communicate, you are just using the wrong register. I know what registers are. I switch registers ten times a day with clients, with partners, with the junior team. That I can understand.

If the homepage had said "Learn to write better prompts" I would have closed the tab. "Notice which mode you are in" does not make me feel stupid.

**6. What would make me send this to a colleague**

If somewhere around module B-06 or B-07, I hit a moment where I thought: "That is why the proposal I wrote last week came back wrong." If it connects to something I have already failed at in my actual job, I will send it to everyone on my team.

The practical guide already gets close. The "everything at once" example — where someone dumps five tasks into one message — is exactly what I did with that proposal. It came back scattered and I blamed the tool. Now I think I was the problem.

**7. What is missing for someone like me**

The single biggest gap: there is no entry point for someone who is not a beginner but is not good at this either. I have been using AI for a month. I know where the text box is. But I do not know what I do not know, so I cannot confidently skip to the right module.

What I need: "Already using AI at work? Start here." A five-question diagnostic, or even a paragraph that says "If you have sent messages and got responses but the results are inconsistent or frustrating, go to B-04."

The guide page is actually closer to what I need right now than B-01 is. The before-and-after prompt pairs are immediately useful. If someone had told me to start at the guide instead of the course, I would have got value out of this in about four minutes.

And: the course has 26 modules. I do not have time for 26 modules. A recommended sequence for people who are already using AI but struggling — five modules, not twenty-six — would change this from "I should probably do this" to "I am doing this tonight."

**8. Honest verdict**

The best thing I have found on this subject. The headline is the first thing I have read in a month about AI that did not make me feel stupid or behind or irrelevant. But it needs a "I already use AI, where do I start?" path, or I will use it myself and not recommend it to colleagues because I cannot tell them where to begin.
