# Linguist — Video Briefs

These are presenter briefs, not scripts. Each brief covers what the video needs to do, the territory to cover, and the moments that should happen. The presenter should read the brief, sit with it, then speak from understanding — not from lines.

---

## Video 1: The Why — The Manifesto

**What this video needs to do**
By the end, the viewer should feel seen — like someone has finally named the specific kind of tired they've been carrying. They should also have a simple, grounding idea to take away: there are two modes, and the problem is forgetting which one you're in.

**The core insight**
The tiredness isn't from the work — it's from being in the wrong mode.

**Content to cover**
- Open with the feeling, not the concept. That particular exhaustion at the end of a day where you achieved things but still feel empty.
- The personal story: the wobble, the two courses, the nine months. What it looked like from outside (success) versus inside (nothing left).
- How AI brought the same pattern back. The machine is always ready, always has energy, always says "what's next?" — and that makes burning out feel like progress.
- The two modes: human mode and machine mode. What each one is, why both are necessary, why neither is wrong.
- The cost of mode bleed: performing human warmth at something that cannot receive it, and then having nothing left for the people who actually need it.
- The skill: it's not a framework, not a course. It's just noticing. Which mode am I in? Is this a machine or a person? That's the whole thing.
- Close with the invitation to experience it at linguist.vuelolabs.com and the promise that this series is practical, not hype.

**Moments that matter**
- The personal story beat. This is the emotional anchor of the whole series — the wobble, what it cost, what it taught. It needs to land as honest, not polished. The presenter should not rush through it.
- The contrast between human mode and machine mode described side by side. Make the difference feel obvious in retrospect — the viewer should think "yes, of course these are completely different things."
- The "hope you're well!" moment — the small, absurd, relatable example of performing politeness at something that can't receive it. This tends to get a laugh of recognition.
- The final landing: once you see it, you can't unsee it. This should feel like a gift, not a pitch.

**How to approach it**
This is the manifesto video — it sets the emotional register for everything that follows. It should feel like a conversation with someone who has thought about this a lot and wants to share what they found, not a lecture. The presenter should resist the urge to sound authoritative; the power is in the honesty. Avoid hype language and AI discourse framing entirely — this is about being human, not about technology.

**Philosophy primer**
The Linguist argument is not really about AI. It's about what happens when you forget which mode you're in. Human mode is warm, circular, expensive — and it's supposed to be expensive, because that's what makes it meaningful. Machine mode is direct, purposeful, and asks nothing of you emotionally. The problem is never the mode itself; it's the bleed — carrying machine mode home, or bringing human performance rituals into machine work. The skill is noticing. That's it. Once you can see the modes, you can choose between them — and that choice is where the energy comes back.

## Video 2: Think / Collab / Dispatch / Iterate

**What this video needs to do**
By the end, the viewer should understand why their AI interactions feel frustrating — and have a simple four-step shape they can actually use next time. The framework should feel obvious once named, like something they were already half-doing wrong.

**The core insight**
Most people skip straight to Dispatch. That's why it feels like the AI doesn't understand them.

**Content to cover**
- Open with the common failure pattern: open it, type something vague, get something mediocre back, feel frustrated, type something else, give up.
- Name the four steps: Think, Collab, Dispatch, Iterate. The order matters.
- Think: before you open the machine, get your own thoughts together. Brain dump on paper, voice memo, back of an envelope. The machine structures chaos well — but it needs your chaos first.
- Collab: the most underused step. Use the machine to figure out what you actually want before you write the brief. Ask it questions; ask it to ask you questions.
- Dispatch: once you know what you want, write a clean instruction and send it. Define the output, format, length, audience. Then walk away.
- Iterate: one or two rounds, then stop. Define "done" before you start. Over-iteration degrades quality — every round takes you further from your original intent.
- Close with a concrete invitation to try it on one real task today, starting with Think.

**Moments that matter**
- A live demonstration of the Collab step: open a real AI tool, type a vague idea, ask it to ask questions one at a time to sharpen the brief. Let the viewer watch the brief get clearer in real time.
- The "make it better" moment: name it as a non-instruction. It has no end state. It invites drift. This lands as a small revelation for most people who have been doing this.
- The contrast between someone who skips straight to Dispatch versus someone who does the full four steps — same task, very different result.

**How to approach it**
This is a practical video, but it shouldn't feel like a tutorial. The presenter should be sharing a framework they actually use, not teaching a lesson. Keep the energy conversational and slightly impatient — the tone should be "this is actually so simple, I want you to see it." The demo moments should feel spontaneous, not rehearsed.

**Philosophy primer**
Machine mode is direct and purposeful — but that directness requires you to know what you want before you arrive. When you skip Think and Collab, you're asking the machine to read your mind, which is a human-mode expectation applied to a machine-mode tool. That mismatch is why it feels frustrating. The four steps are really about doing the human thinking first — getting clear in the messy, circular way that humans actually think — so that when you arrive at the machine, you're ready to give it instructions, not a half-formed feeling.

## Video 3: Human Mode — Protecting What Matters

**What this video needs to do**
By the end, the viewer should be able to recognise mode bleed in their own life — and have one small, concrete thing to try. The video should feel like an honest conversation about something that doesn't get talked about enough.

**The core insight**
You have a finite human battery. A day in machine mode empties it. The people in your life pay for that.

**Content to cover**
- Open with the specific cost: not burnout from hard work, but arriving home still in machine mode.
- What human mode actually is: warm, circular, inefficient in a good way. You explain the same thing three different ways because that's how humans build shared understanding. You pause, you feel, you react. It's been working for millions of years.
- Where it gets contaminated: a day of AI work trains you toward short answers, task focus, no padding. You come home and you're still there — "what's the output? what's the timeline?" — to the people who needed something completely different.
- It bleeds in both directions: being too machine with humans at work too. Bullet-pointing conversations that need warmth. Putting time limits on disagreements. Treating emotional requests like structured tasks.
- How to come back: it's a switch, and you can flip it deliberately. Simple transitions — closing the laptop, arriving at a conversation, asking how someone is and actually wanting to know, letting the answer go wherever it goes.
- Close with one small thing: notice which mode you're in when you arrive home. Just notice. You don't have to change it yet.

**Moments that matter**
- A concrete, slightly absurd example of machine-mode bleed in a human conversation — the friend asking for a lift at 2am and getting a structured request format back, or giving your kid a status update when they asked how you're feeling.
- The "the machine doesn't know you've left — your family does" idea. Let this land quietly. Don't over-explain it.
- A personal moment, if the presenter has one, of catching themselves in machine mode with someone they love. Vulnerability here builds trust.
- The landing on "human mode doesn't need to be efficient — that's the point." This is the reframe that stays with people.

**How to approach it**
This is the most personal video in the series. The presenter should let that be true — not manufacture emotion, but not shy away from the real weight of the subject either. The tone is honest and warm. Avoid productivity framing entirely: this is not about optimising your human relationships. It's about protecting them.

**Philosophy primer**
Human mode is expensive by design. The circularity, the inefficiency, the pauses — these aren't flaws in human communication, they're the mechanism by which trust and meaning actually get built. When you bleed machine mode into human interactions, you're not just being slightly cold — you're using the wrong tool for the most important work in your life. The cost isn't always visible immediately. It accumulates. And the antidote isn't a technique; it's noticing. Which mode am I in right now? Is this a machine or a person? The switch is already there. You just have to remember to use it.

## Video 4: Machine Mode — Getting Your Input Right

**What this video needs to do**
By the end, the viewer should know exactly what goes into a good machine-mode instruction — and feel capable of writing one. The four elements should feel like a shape they can use immediately, not a framework to memorise.

**The core insight**
The output is only as good as the input, and most people have never been shown what good input actually looks like.

**Content to cover**
- Open with the honest observation: the frustrating interactions are almost never the AI's fault. They're almost always an input problem.
- Introduce the four elements: Objective, Context, Role, Constraints. These are the things every good instruction contains.
- Objective: be specific about what done looks like before you start. Output format, length, audience — all of this belongs in the objective, not implied by it.
- Context: a precision tool, not a life story. What does it need to know to do this well? Only that. Too little and the output is vague. Too much and it gets lost.
- Role: give it a lens. "You are a senior consultant who has done 200 of these" changes the tone, the assumptions, and the quality of the output.
- Constraints: one thing at a time, every time. Scope creep in the prompt produces scope creep in the output. You can also give it interaction instructions — "ask me one question at a time" is a legitimate instruction.
- Live build: start with a real, messy thought. Walk through Think, then Collab to sharpen it, then Dispatch with the clean prompt — building all four elements in real time.
- Close with the invitation to try the four elements on the next real interaction.

**Moments that matter**
- A side-by-side comparison: the same request, with and without a Role. The difference in output quality should be visible and a little surprising.
- The live build from messy thought to clean dispatch. This is the most useful demonstration in the series — it should feel real, not polished.
- The "if you can't describe the output, you can't ask for it" idea. Simple, but it stops a lot of people in their tracks.

**How to approach it**
This is the most practical and technical video in the series, but it should still feel like someone sharing how they actually work, not a how-to guide. The presenter should be concrete and specific — examples matter more than explanations here. Keep the energy efficient; this is the machine-mode video, and the presenter can model that directness.

**Philosophy primer**
Machine mode asks one thing of you: clarity. The machine cannot infer, cannot feel what you meant, cannot read between the lines. When you arrive at a machine with a vague instruction, you're using human-mode behaviour — the expectation that shared context and warmth will carry meaning — in a context where those things don't exist. The four elements are just a way of being precise: here is what I want, here is what you need to know, here is who you should be, here is when we're done. That's not learning AI. That's learning to speak machine.

## Video 5: Output, Storage and the Art of Stopping

**What this video needs to do**
By the end, the viewer should understand that the output is the beginning, not the end — and have a concrete system for capturing what they build so they don't start from scratch every time.

**The core insight**
Most people use AI like a disposable lighter. You're not building anything. Here's how to start.

**Content to cover**
- Open with the disposable-lighter pattern: open it, get something, use it, throw it away, start from scratch next time.
- What to do with the output: read it, actually read it. Make it yours — adapt the tone, the specifics, the parts only you could know. If you can't explain what it produced, you didn't really understand what you asked.
- Done is done: define done before you start. First good version is usually the best. "Make it better" is not an instruction. Over-iteration takes you away from the clarity that made the first draft work.
- Building a context document: your name, role, tone of voice, recurring formats, things it should always and never do. Paste it at the start of every new conversation. Stop re-introducing yourself from scratch.
- Building a project: context plus goal plus a set of outputs that build on each other. Think of it like a brief for a smart contractor. The machine is the contractor. The brief matters.
- Reusable patterns: for recurring tasks, build a template once and reuse it. The goal is to think less every time you do the same thing.
- Close: this week, build one context document. One page, plain text, six to eight lines.

**Moments that matter**
- A demonstration of a simple context document — show what it looks like, paste it into a conversation, show how the output changes. This should be demystifying: it's just a plain text document.
- The "if you wouldn't put your name on it, don't send it" moment. This lands as a quiet accountability check that reframes the whole relationship with the output.
- The contrast between starting from scratch every time versus having a context document — the cumulative cost of the former should feel real by now.

**How to approach it**
This video should feel like the series clicking into a practical gear — the viewer has the philosophy from Videos 1–3 and the input skills from Video 4, and now they're building something that persists. The energy here is calm and constructive. The presenter should make the system feel achievable — one page, plain text, start simple. This is not about sophistication.

**Philosophy primer**
Machine mode is not just about individual interactions — it's about building something that gets better over time. Every time you start from scratch, you're treating the machine like a vending machine rather than a contractor you've briefed. The context document is the brief. It encodes what you know about what you need, so you don't have to burn cognitive energy re-establishing it every session. This is how you stop spending yourself on things the machine could just carry. And that saved energy — that's what gets returned to the human side of your life.

## Video 6: Building Bandwidth — A System That Works for You

**What this video needs to do**
By the end, the viewer should feel like they've finished something — a series that started with philosophy and ends with a working system. They should leave with a clear picture of what to build and why it matters beyond productivity.

**The core insight**
The goal isn't to get good at AI once. It's to build a system that keeps reducing the load — so you have more of yourself for the things that actually need you.

**Content to cover**
- Open with the reframe: expertise isn't doing something well, it's doing it well without burning bandwidth.
- What bandwidth actually is: a finite daily resource. Every decision costs it. Good systems reduce the number of new decisions you need to make. The goal is to protect your thinking for the things that deserve it.
- The personal system: three components — context document, template library, recurring formats. Simple and consistent beats sophisticated and abandoned.
- Talking to the machine to build the system: ask it "what would help you help me better?" Ask it to interview you and build your context document. Let it ask one question at a time. Save the result. Use it forever.
- Where this leads: recurring tasks become dispatches, not decisions. The machine starts to feel like a team member with context — because it has context. More output, less of you going into it.
- Protecting what matters: everything in this series has been in service of one thing — getting more done with less of yourself, so you have more of yourself left for the things that need it. The relationships. The creativity. The rest. The slow stuff that only works when you're actually present.
- Close: link to linguist.vuelolabs.com. "This is the start. More coming." End where the series began.

**Moments that matter**
- A live demonstration of asking the machine to interview you for your own context document — questions one at a time, building the document in real time. This is the most memorable and practical demo in the series.
- A brief, honest look at what a minimal working system actually looks like — one context doc, three templates, a folder. Make it feel achievable, not aspirational.
- The closing moment: the return to the philosophy. Let the machine be a machine. That frees you to be a human. This should land as a conclusion, not a tagline.

**How to approach it**
This video is the culmination of the series — it should feel like arrival, not summary. The presenter should bring the fullness of everything covered so far: the philosophy, the practical skills, the system. The energy is warm and honest, not triumphant. The close should feel like a real ending — not a sales moment, but a genuine "go be human" from someone who means it.

**Philosophy primer**
The whole Linguist argument comes to a point here: the machine scales infinitely, you don't, and the system is what bridges that gap — not so you can do more, but so you can spend less of yourself on the things that don't need you. Every template, every context document, every saved pattern is a small act of protecting your human bandwidth for the things that actually run on it: relationships, creativity, rest, presence. The machine will always look for more instructions. It will wait forever. It does not mind. You are the one who minds. Build the system so you can stop minding — and go be somewhere else.
