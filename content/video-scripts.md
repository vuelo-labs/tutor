## Video 2: Think / Collab / Dispatch / Iterate

**Core message:** Most people skip straight to Dispatch. That's why it's frustrating.

---

### HOOK (0:00–0:30)
"Most people use AI like this. Open it. Type something vague. Get something mediocre back. Feel frustrated. Type something else. Feel more frustrated. Close it and decide it's overhyped."

[SB] "The problem isn't the AI. The problem is the four steps you skipped."

---

### THE FRAMEWORK INTRO (0:30–1:30)
- Think. Collab. Dispatch. Iterate.
- Four steps. Most people only do one. Sometimes zero.
- Walk through each one. The order matters.

---

### THINK (1:30–3:00)
- Before you open the machine, get your own thoughts together.
- Doesn't have to be perfect. Brain dump on paper. Voice memo to yourself. Back of an envelope.
- The machine is brilliant at structuring chaos — but it needs *your* chaos first.
- If you skip Think, you arrive at the machine with a half-formed idea and expect it to read your mind.

[SB] "Garbage in, garbage out. But also: vague in, vague out."

[CUT]

---

### COLLAB (3:00–4:30)
- This is the most underused step. Use the machine to figure out what you actually want.
- You don't have to know the answer going in. That's what Collab is for.
- Ask it questions. Ask it to ask *you* questions. Use it to sharpen the brief before you write the brief.
- "I have this problem, help me figure out what I'm actually asking."

[DEMO: Open Claude. Type a vague idea. Ask it to "ask me one question at a time to help me define what I actually need." Walk through 3-4 questions. Show how the brief sharpens.]

[SB] "You don't have to know exactly what you want going in. That's what Collab is for."

[CUT — demo clip works as a standalone]

---

### DISPATCH (4:30–5:45)
- Now you know what you want. Write a clean instruction and send it.
- You don't need to watch it work. Walk away. The machine doesn't need supervision.
- Done means: you've defined the output, the format, the length, the audience.
- Send it. Go do something else.

[SB] "Dispatch and disappear. Check back when it's done."

---

### ITERATE (5:45–7:15)
- One or two rounds. Then stop.
- This is the one people get wrong most often.
- Quality degrades with over-iteration. Every round takes you further from your original objective.
- "Make it better" is not an instruction. It's an invitation to drift.
- Define done *before* you start. "This is done when it's under 200 words, has one clear call to action, and uses no jargon."
- When you get there, stop.

[SB] "Infinite iteration is a losing game. It feels like progress. It isn't."

[CUT]

---

### CLOSE (7:15–8:00)
- Try this with one real task today. Start with Think. Don't skip it.
- The rest of this series goes deeper on each mode.
- Link to linguist.vuelolabs.com/guide for the written version.

## Video 3: Human Mode — Protecting What Matters

**Core message:** You have a finite human battery. Here's where it's leaking.

---

### HOOK (0:00–0:30)
"I want to tell you about the most expensive conversation you're probably having right now. It's not the hard one with your boss. It's not the difficult client. It's the two hours every evening you spend in machine mode with the people you love."

[SB] "You come home. And you've got nothing left. And it's not because you worked hard. It's because you worked in the wrong mode all day."

---

### WHAT HUMAN MODE IS (0:30–2:00)
- Warm. Circular. Inefficient in a good way.
- You explain the same thing three different ways because that's how humans build shared understanding.
- You pause. You feel. You react. Sometimes you just sit with it.
- This has been working for millions of years. It still works. It's not a bug. It's the whole design.

[SB] "Human mode is expensive. It's *supposed* to be expensive. That's what makes it meaningful."

---

### WHERE IT GETS CONTAMINATED (2:00–4:00)
- A day of AI work puts you in machine mode.
- Short answers. Task-focused. Efficient. No padding.
- You come home and you're still there: "What do you need? What's the output? What's the timeline?"
- To your partner. To your kids. To your friends.
- It's not intentional. But it's real. And people feel it.

[SB] "The machine doesn't know you've left. Your family does."

[CUT]

---

### IT BLEEDS BOTH WAYS (4:00–5:30)
- Also happens at work: being too machine with humans.
- Bullet-pointing conversations that need warmth. Putting time limits on disagreements.
- Your friend asking you for a lift to the airport at 2am — and you respond with a structured request format.
- "Your friend is not a REST API."

[SB] "Some problems aren't structured. That's what makes them human."

---

### HOW TO COME BACK (5:30–7:30)
- It's a switch. You can flip it deliberately.
- Simple things: a transition between machine work and human time.
- Leave the laptop. Put the phone down. Arrive at the conversation.
- Ask "how are you?" — and actually want to know.
- Let the answer go wherever it goes. You don't need to fix it or optimise it.

[SB] "Human mode doesn't need to be efficient. That's the point."

[CUT]

---

### CLOSE (7:30–8:00)
- One thing today: notice which mode you're in when you arrive home.
- You don't have to change it yet. Just notice.

## Video 4: Machine Mode — Getting Your Input Right

**Core message:** The output is only as good as the input. Here's what good input looks like.

---

### HOOK (0:00–0:30)
"The difference between a useful AI interaction and a frustrating one is almost never the AI. It's almost always what you put in."

[SB] "You get back what you put in. But most people have never been shown what to put in."

---

### THE FOUR ELEMENTS (0:30–2:00)
Quick intro: four things, every time.
1. **Objective** — what you actually want
2. **Context** — only what's relevant
3. **Role** — who it should be for this task
4. **Constraints** — format, length, audience, done criteria

Most people only include one of these. Sometimes zero.

---

### OBJECTIVE (2:00–3:00)
- "Write an email" vs "Write a 3-sentence follow-up email to a client who hasn't responded in 10 days. Professional but warm. No apologies."
- Be specific about what done looks like before you start.
- Output format. Length. Audience. All of these belong in the objective.

[SB] "If you can't describe the output, you can't ask for it."

---

### CONTEXT (3:00–4:00)
- Context is a precision tool. Not a life story.
- What does it *need* to know to do this well? Only that. Nothing else.
- Too little context: vague output. Too much context: it gets lost.
- Goldilocks principle.

[SB] "Give it what it needs. Not everything you know."

[CUT]

---

### ROLE (4:00–5:00)
- "You are a senior consultant who has done 200 of these presentations."
- Changes the tone, the assumptions, the quality — completely.
- It's not magic. It's calibration.

[DEMO: Same request, with and without a role. Show the difference in output quality side by side.]

[SB] "Give it a personality. It'll give you a better answer."

[CUT — demo is a great short]

---

### CONSTRAINTS (5:00–6:00)
- One thing at a time. Every time. Even if you have five things.
- Ask one thing, get a result, then the next thing.
- Scope creep in the prompt = scope creep in the output.
- "Ask me one question at a time" — you can give it interaction instructions. Use them.

[SB] "One thing at a time. Sounds simple. Changes everything."

---

### LIVE BUILD (6:00–7:30)
[DEMO: Start with a real messy thought. Think step — brain dump out loud. Collab step — ask Claude to sharpen it. Dispatch — show the clean prompt. Walk through the four elements as you build it.]

---

### CLOSE (7:30–8:00)
- Try the four elements on your next interaction.
- Next video: what to do with the output — storage, projects, and knowing when to stop.

## Video 5: Output, Storage and the Art of Stopping

**Core message:** You're not building anything. Here's how to start.

---

### HOOK (0:00–0:30)
"Most people use AI like a disposable lighter. Spark, use, throw away. Start from scratch every time. Wonder why it never seems to improve."

[SB] "You're not building anything. You're just burning matches."

[CUT]

---

### WHAT TO DO WITH THE OUTPUT (0:30–2:00)
- The output isn't the end. It's the start.
- Read it. Actually read it.
- Make it yours — adapt the tone, the specifics, the parts only you could know.
- Don't copy-paste without reading. If you can't explain what it produced, you didn't understand what you asked.

[SB] "If you wouldn't put your name on it, don't send it."

---

### DONE IS DONE (2:00–3:30)
- Define done before you start (callback to Video 4 constraints).
- First good version is usually the best.
- Every iteration round takes you further from your original intent.
- "Make it better" is not an instruction. It has no end state.
- Over-iterated work loses the thing that made the first draft good: clarity.

[SB] "The longer you iterate, the worse it gets. Stop while it's good."

[CUT]

---

### STORING RESULTS (3:30–5:00)
- Build a context document. Start simple.
- Your name, role, tone of voice, recurring formats, things it should always/never do.
- Paste it in at the start of a new conversation.
- Stop explaining yourself from scratch every time.

[DEMO: Show a simple context document — one page, plain text, 6-8 bullet points. Paste it into Claude. Show how the output changes.]

[SB] "Stop re-introducing yourself to every conversation. Build a document that does it for you."

---

### BUILDING A PROJECT (5:00–6:30)
- A project = context + goal + set of outputs that build on each other.
- Think of it like a brief you'd give a smart contractor.
- The machine is the contractor. You're the client. The brief matters.
- Keep it in one place. Add to it. Reference back to it.

[SB] "You're not prompting. You're managing a project."

[CUT]

---

### REUSABLE PATTERNS (6:30–7:30)
- Recurring tasks: build a template once, reuse forever.
- Monday update? Build the template. Fill it in. Dispatch.
- Weekly report, client follow-up, meeting summary — all of these can become 30-second tasks.
- Goal: think less every time you do the same thing.

[SB] "The goal is to think less about the things that don't deserve your thinking."

---

### CLOSE (7:30–8:00)
- This week: build your first context document. One page. Your name, your role, your tone, your top 3 recurring tasks.
- Next video: building a system that runs without you thinking about it.

## Video 6: Building Bandwidth — A System That Works for You

**Core message:** The goal isn't to get good at AI once. It's to build something that keeps reducing the load.

---

### HOOK (0:00–0:30)
"The goal isn't to get good at using AI once. It's to build a system so that you barely have to think about it."

[SB] "Expertise isn't doing something well. It's doing it well without burning bandwidth."

---

### WHAT BANDWIDTH ACTUALLY IS (0:30–2:00)
- Cognitive bandwidth is a finite daily resource.
- Every decision costs it. Every new thing costs it.
- Good systems reduce the number of new decisions you need to make.
- The goal: protect your thinking for the things that deserve it.

[SB] "Every small decision you automate is energy you keep for something that matters."

[CUT]

---

### YOUR PERSONAL SYSTEM (2:00–4:00)
- Three things: your context document, your template library, your recurring formats.
- Doesn't have to be complicated. A folder. A doc. Something consistent.
- Simple and consistent beats sophisticated and abandoned. Every time.

[DEMO: Walk through a real minimal system — one context doc, three simple templates for recurring tasks. Show how quickly a Monday update becomes a 2-minute task.]

[SB] "Simple and consistent beats sophisticated and abandoned. Every time."

---

### TALK TO IT DIRECTLY (4:00–5:30)
- You can have an actual conversation with the machine to build your system.
- Ask it: "What would help you help me better?"
- Ask it: "Ask me one question at a time and help me write my context document."
- Let it interview you. Save the result. Use it every time.
- This is generalisable — every AI tool, same principle.

[DEMO: Open Claude. "I want to build a context document so you can give me better results. Ask me one question at a time." Walk through 5-6 questions live. Show the resulting document.]

- The machine starts to feel like a team member with context — because it has context.
- More output, less of you going into it.

[SB] "The machine scales. You don't. The system is what bridges that gap."

- Getting more done with less of yourself, so you have more of yourself for the things that need it.
- The relationships. The creativity. The rest. The slow stuff that only works when you're actually present.

[SB] "Let the machine be a machine. That frees you to be a human."
